🎯 Key Findings from SMC Data

1. Group Size Has Minimal Impact on Consensus

Consensus probability remains remarkably stable across population sizes: 91.7-92.8% for 0% zealots. The variation is less than 2%, indicating group size has minimal effect on consensus achievement.

2. Zealot Percentage is the Dominant Factor

All population sizes show complete consensus breakdown at 20% zealots. The critical factor is the proportion of zealots, not the absolute population size.

3. Larger Groups Show Earlier Sensitivity

Larger populations (N≥150) show consensus degradation starting at 15% zealots, while smaller groups maintain performance until 20%. This suggests larger groups are slightly more sensitive to zealot influence.

4. Consistent Performance Window

All group sizes maintain excellent consensus (>92%) up to 10% zealots, providing a reliable operating range regardless of population size.

⚖️ Zealot Threshold Analysis

Critical Observations:

  • Universal 20% Threshold: All population sizes reach deadlock at 20% zealots
  • 15% Sensitivity Point: Larger groups (N≥100) start showing degradation at 15%
  • Safe Operating Range: 0-10% zealots provide consistent performance across all sizes
  • Size-Independent Pattern: Zealot percentage, not absolute count, determines consensus fate

Practical Implications: System designers should focus on controlling zealot ratios rather than optimizing population size for consensus probability.

🎯 Conclusion: Group Size vs Consensus Probability

Group size N has minimal impact on consensus probability within the tested range (N=50-200).

Consensus probability remains stable around 92% regardless of population size when zealot percentages are low (0-10%). The critical determinant of consensus success is the proportion of zealots, not the absolute group size.

Design Recommendation: Choose group size based on computational resources, speed requirements, and fault tolerance needs rather than consensus probability optimization.
